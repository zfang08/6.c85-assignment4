Dataset and Topic: Civilian Complaints Against New York City Police Officers.

  • This is a dataset compiled by ProPublica, an independent, nonprofit investigative journalism newsroom. It contains more than 12,000 civilian complaints filed against the NYPD, with demographic information about the complainant and officer, the category of the alleged misconduct, and the result of the complaint.
  • The dataset consists of 33,358 records and 27 variables detailing civilian complaints against NYPD officers.

Proposition: "Complaints against NYPD officers are rarely substantiated, making police accountability ineffective."

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • I chose sankey diagram to show proportion. Particularly, we see that the majority of complaints are by police officers and detective, and they fall under abuse of authority , and force.
  • I chose to represent the portion which is substantiated in green, and the unsubstantiated and exonerated both in red. This call attention to the fact that large proporition of allegations remain unsubstantiated. This grouping of unsubstantiated and exonerated tries to deceive the user into comparing substantiated vs. unsubstantiated.
  • I chose the colors deliberately to create a contrast between accountability and dismissal. Green is commonly associated with success, validation, or progress, making substantiated complaints appear small but important. In contrast, red is often linked to warnings, failures, or injustice, emphasizing the overwhelming share of complaints that do not lead to discipline. This color selection subtly reinforces the argument that the system overwhelmingly dismisses complaints rather than holding officers accountable。

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • I chose a line chart to emphasize the trend over time. Unlike the Sankey diagram, which focuses on proportions, the line chart highlights how substantiation rates have changed over the years, providing a temporal perspective on police accountability. This format allows the viewer to see whether the system has improved or remained stagnant over time.
  • I included a regression trend line to make the increasing trend more visible. While the year-over-year fluctuations might make it harder to see a clear pattern, the trend line reinforces the idea that substantiation rates are gradually rising. This helps counter the argument that police accountability has remained ineffective. Instead we see that accountability has improved over the years.
  • I used green to represent substantiated complaints, reinforcing a positive narrative. Since green is commonly associated with success.

Final Reflection

Going through this process, I realized how much power visualization has in shaping a narrative. The Sankey diagram made it easy to highlight the sheer number of complaints that remain unsubstantiated or exonerated, pushing the idea that police accountability is weak. On the other hand, the line chart told a very different story—by showing a slow but steady increase in substantiation rates, it suggested that accountability is improving over time. Some parts of this were pretty straightforward, like picking colors and choosing chart types that best fit the argument. But what was tricky was figuring out where to draw the line between persuasion and manipulation. Small choices, like grouping unsubstantiated and exonerated cases together in red or using a trend line to smooth out fluctuations, made a big difference in how the data was interpreted. What surprised me the most was how easy it is to nudge people toward a certain conclusion without technically lying. I wouldn’t have thought that just changing the way categories are grouped or adjusting color choices could make the same dataset feel completely different. This made me rethink what ethical visualization really means. It’s not just about presenting accurate numbers—it’s also about being honest with how those numbers are framed. Some persuasive choices, like emphasizing important trends, feel fair, but others, like hiding key context or exaggerating differences, start to cross the line into misleading territory. At the end of the day, intent matters. If a visualization is designed to help people understand data, that’s fine. But if it’s designed to push a specific agenda while hiding nuance, that’s where it starts to feel dishonest.
